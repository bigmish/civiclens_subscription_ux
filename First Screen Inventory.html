Competitive Audit · June 2026

First Screen Inventory

How 10 complex, high-volume products introduce their value to users who may not yet know what the product can do — or how to use it.

Five essential needs of a first screen
1
Fit Confirmation
"Is this for me?" — the user needs to quickly recognise that this product is relevant to their context before investing any setup energy.
2
Value Comprehension
"What will I be able to do?" — the screen should communicate a concrete outcome the user can expect, not just a feature list.
3
Path to Value
"How long before I see results?" — users are doing a subconscious ROI calculation. The screen should signal that the payoff is near.
4
Clear First Action
"What do I do right now?" — one unambiguous step forward, low enough friction that the user acts without overthinking.
5
Permission to Explore
"Can I skip this and come back?" — expert and returning users need an escape hatch. Forcing a linear path alienates the already-converted.

Airtable

AI Personalization Wizard
Leads with an AI assistant persona — "Omni" — before the user sees any product UI. Context collection happens through conversation.
1 · Fit Confirmation
Asking for company name and role implies business users, but doesn't confirm product relevance before the user commits to the form.
2 · Value Comprehension
"I'll create powerful tools, get insights, and automate your work" directly names outcomes. The AI framing signals intelligent automation, not just a spreadsheet replacement.
3 · Path to Value
No indication of how long setup takes or when the user will see their first useful result. The wizard extends before the product is revealed.
4 · Clear First Action
A single labeled input with one button. The path forward is completely unambiguous — the best clarity of action in the set.
5 · Permission to Explore
No skip or bypass available. The wizard must be completed before the user sees any product UI.
Overall verdict
Strong clarity of action and solid value framing, but the sequence is inverted — users commit before they've seen anything. Works best when Airtable is already a trusted brand at the decision point.

02

Notion

Use-Case Survey → Live Document
A brief role-selection survey routes users into a personalized "Getting Started" page — which itself demonstrates Notion's format through hands-on exploration.
1 · Fit Confirmation
The use-case cards (For myself / For my team / For my company) let users self-identify their context directly before setup begins.
2 · Value Comprehension
The Getting Started page is itself a Notion document — users grasp the product's format by experiencing it, not reading about it. Shown, not explained.
3 · Path to Value
Personalized templates give a sense of what's possible quickly, but there's no explicit signal of how fast a useful workspace can be built.
4 · Clear First Action
One choice to make (use case), then a live document to explore. Low ambiguity at each step — the first action is the product itself.
5 · Permission to Explore
No forced tour. After the brief survey, users move at their own pace through a fully functional document. Freedom from minute one.
Overall verdict
One of the strongest performances in the set. The live-document approach elegantly solves the blank-canvas problem, and the survey personalizes without overwhelming. The one gap: the survey assumes you already know what Notion is.

03

Figma

Dashboard + Opt-in Tour Modal
Drops users directly into the real product — a dashboard of files — and offers an optional guided tour. Expert users can skip immediately; newcomers are offered a structured walkthrough.
1 · Fit Confirmation
No fit check. The empty dashboard assumes users know they belong here. Someone unfamiliar with design tools gets no signal that Figma is for them.
2 · Value Comprehension
The welcome modal briefly differentiates Figma from competitors ("key differences from other tools"), but for users who've never used a design tool, the product's potential remains abstract.
3 · Path to Value
After the modal, there's no indication of how long it takes to create something meaningful, or what a "win" looks like on day one.
4 · Clear First Action
The modal offers a single binary choice: take the tour or skip. Low friction, immediately actionable — the best aspect of this first screen.
5 · Permission to Explore
The opt-in tour is a textbook example of respecting the user's existing knowledge. Expert users dismiss and start immediately; newcomers can choose the guided path.
Overall verdict
Highly respectful of experienced users, but leaves newcomers without scaffolding. Closing the modal reveals an empty dashboard with no obvious next step — the gap between "welcome" and "create" is unbridged.

04

Slack

Workspace Setup Wizard
New workspace creators go through a step-by-step setup flow collecting team name, use case, and teammate invitations — before entering the actual product.
1 · Fit Confirmation
The workspace naming screen assumes commitment without showing the product. Users are asked to name something they haven't yet seen.
2 · Value Comprehension
The product is completely invisible during setup. No channels, no messages, no conversation — until the wizard is complete there is nothing to evaluate.
3 · Path to Value
Three steps, then a first message sent. The wizard is short and culminates in a direct activation moment — the user experiences the product working almost immediately.
4 · Clear First Action
A single field at each step with clear forward momentum. The progression is unambiguous — one of the cleanest step-by-step flows in the set.
5 · Permission to Explore
No skip option during setup. Notably, invitees — the majority of real new users — encounter an entirely different, much less guided entry path with no wizard at all.
Overall verdict
Exceptionally well-paced once the user is committed, but the pre-show ask is a gamble — users configure before experiencing anything. Works because Slack's brand is already trusted at the decision point.

05

HubSpot

Setup Checklist
Greets new users with a task-based checklist of setup steps — connect email, import contacts, create a deal — rather than the product UI. Progress drives forward momentum.
1 · Fit Confirmation
No fit check. The checklist assumes the user is already a CRM user. It configures the product without confirming whether it's the right tool for their situation.
2 · Value Comprehension
Checklist items (contacts, deals, emails) implicitly map to CRM capabilities — but there's no explicit outcome framing. Users understand the inputs, not yet the results they'll get.
3 · Path to Value
The progress bar is explicit: 4 steps, 1 complete, 3 to go. Users have a clear mental model of what remains and a sense that the finish line is near.
4 · Clear First Action
The active step is highlighted and labeled. No ambiguity about where to start — the checklist structure eliminates the "where do I even begin?" problem common in complex CRMs.
5 · Permission to Explore
Individual steps can be skipped or deferred, but the checklist frame itself is presented as mandatory scaffolding before the full product is accessible.
Overall verdict
Excellent at eliminating the "where do I start?" problem through structure, but entirely task-oriented rather than outcome-oriented. Users leave the checklist knowing how to set up HubSpot — not what they'll actually be able to do with it.

06

Monday.com

Template Gallery
"What would you like to manage?" — a use-case gallery of template categories displayed as visual tiles. Users select a domain and are dropped into a pre-populated board.
1 · Fit Confirmation
The template gallery directly answers "is this for my team?" — 8 categories covering marketing, sales, HR, IT and more. Users recognise their context immediately.
2 · Value Comprehension
Visual template tiles communicate the product's range faster than any copy. Users see what a Monday board looks like — and what it can organise — before committing to setup.
3 · Path to Value
Selecting a template drops users into a pre-populated working board immediately — the fastest path to a useful state in this entire set. No blank canvas.
4 · Clear First Action
A single selection from a gallery. The choice is clear and low stakes — the interface explicitly signals "you can always change this later."
5 · Permission to Explore
Users can browse all categories freely before committing. The "Other" tile is a safety valve for edge cases and signals the system won't box you in.
Overall verdict
The strongest first screen of the set for value introduction. The template gallery solves fit confirmation, value comprehension, and fast activation simultaneously. The main risk is choice paralysis when too many templates look similar.

07

GitHub

Role & Interest Personalization
A short survey asking for role, experience level, and areas of interest — used to personalize the GitHub feed and surface relevant repositories and learning content.
1 · Fit Confirmation
Role selection (Developer, Designer, Student, etc.) lets users self-identify, but the framing is about personalising a feed — not confirming whether GitHub is the right tool for this person's goals.
2 · Value Comprehension
The survey form shows nothing about what GitHub enables. A first-time user completing the form still has no concrete sense of what they can build, share, or collaborate on.
3 · Path to Value
After the survey, users land on a personalised feed — but there's no explicit path to the most common first action: creating a repository. The distance to "done" is unclear.
4 · Clear First Action
Selecting a role chip and clicking Continue is simple and quick. The UI is clean and unambiguous at each step of the survey.
5 · Permission to Explore
The survey can be completed quickly, and the resulting dashboard is browsable — but there's no explicit "skip personalisation" option for users who just want to get to work.
Overall verdict
Optimised for the already-converted. For developers who know GitHub, the personalisation is a useful nicety. For newcomers, the first screen provides no glimpse of the product's core value — collaborative code hosting — before asking for information.

08

Linear

Minimal Workspace Creation
A single dark screen: name your workspace, get a URL, continue. No survey, no tour, no explanation. Linear bets its users are technical enough to find their own way in.
1 · Fit Confirmation
No explicit fit check. The dark minimalist aesthetic signals "developer tool" to the right audience, but makes no direct claim about who this is for or what problem it solves.
2 · Value Comprehension
Workspace creation communicates nothing about what Linear enables. Users name a workspace with no preview of what the product looks like or what their team can accomplish with it.
3 · Path to Value
No sense of setup time, what the product looks like, or what a team achieves in their first week. The payoff is entirely deferred until after workspace creation.
4 · Clear First Action
A single text field, one button. The most friction-free first action in the entire set — perfectly aligned with Linear's "speed first" product philosophy.
5 · Permission to Explore
After workspace creation, users enter the product directly with no forced tour or wizard. Full freedom from the start — consistent with the tool's developer-first values.
Overall verdict
A deliberate bet on self-selection: Linear assumes its users are software engineers who already know what they want. This works within that audience but is entirely opaque outside it — Linear's exceptional speed and UX are invisible until you're already inside.

09

Webflow

Use-Case Intent Selection
"What are you here to do?" — three or four intent options that branch the experience before the user ever sees the designer. The goal: route users to the right template and starting context.
1 · Fit Confirmation
Three intent options (Design a site / Build client projects / Explore & learn) segment users by context and allow self-routing before any setup begins.
2 · Value Comprehension
The options name activities but don't show the visual designer — Webflow's most distinctive capability. Users choose a path without seeing where it leads or what they'll be able to create.
3 · Path to Value
No indication of setup time or what the user will be able to publish. The options promise directions but not destinations — the payoff is deferred with no timeline signal.
4 · Clear First Action
Three clearly labelled choices with short descriptors. Simple decision, low stakes, with an explicit no-commitment escape hatch ("Explore & learn").
5 · Permission to Explore
"Explore & learn" is an explicit no-commitment option — respectful of users who aren't ready to commit to a project type and want to understand the tool first.
Overall verdict
Smart routing that deserves credit, but Webflow's biggest wow moment — the visual designer — is entirely hidden behind the selection screen. Showing a preview of the designer within one of the option cards would dramatically improve value comprehension.

10

Jira

Project Type & Template Selection
Asks users to create a project immediately by choosing a methodology — Scrum, Kanban, or Bug Tracking — with visual board previews. Structural commitment before any orientation.
1 · Fit Confirmation
No fit check at all. Users are immediately asked to create a project with a methodology — with no framing of whether Jira is the right tool or whether it suits their team's way of working.
2 · Value Comprehension
Board preview thumbnails give a first glimpse of the product UI, but the abstract empty columns convey structure without conveying what a team achieves by using it.
3 · Path to Value
No indication of setup time, what a well-configured Jira looks like, or what the team will be able to do once the board is running. The payoff is entirely implicit.
4 · Clear First Action
The project type selection is visually clear, but "Scrum vs. Kanban" is methodology jargon. Teams without agile experience may feel paralysed by a choice they don't yet understand.
5 · Permission to Explore
No skip or browse option. Users must commit to a project methodology before accessing any other part of the product — a high-stakes decision made with no context.
Overall verdict
Designed for teams already familiar with agile — likely a migration from an older Jira version or a competitor. For genuine first-timers, the methodology-first approach creates a high-friction, jargon-heavy decision that sets the wrong tone for a tool meant to reduce complexity.
